Authors' Notes

I wanted to write a sequel to "The World's Tallest Cryptic" from 2008, and sometime in the spring I thought of doing one in 2D this time, using Braille, so I asked for a suitably small answer. When I got it, I realized that it could be made by infinitely reflecting a 5x3 grid:

XXXOX
XOOXX
OOXOX

So I could make a cycle-5 "clock" on top out of 5 "bit flows" (chains of two-option entries) which rotated their relative positions when passing from one top grid to the next, and similarly make a cycle-3 "clock" on the left. ("The World's Tallest Cryptic" had had a similar cycle-5 clock and a cycle-3 clock running through all its grids, bottom to top.) From the clocks, bit flows T1, T2, T3, T4, T5 could run downwards (with Tn indicating that you are in stage n of the top cycle) and L1, L2, and L3 rightwards, to tell each "main" grid where it was in each cycle.

I later realized that if T1, T2, and T3 rotated their relative positions as they went downwards, the logic in each main grid would look like:

XXXOX
OXOXX
OXOOX

and would need only four multi-input logic gates: You would have an O exactly when ((T4 and not L2) or ((T1 or T3) and not L1)).

The construction itself took (parts of) 8 days (compared with 11 for "The World's Tallest Cryptic"): I guessed that an 8x8 main grid would have enough space (it would need to pass along T1, T2, and T3 rotated, and T4, L1, and L2 unrotated, and implement 4 gates), found a suitable 8x8 bar arrangement, and worked out the main grid fill starting at the magenta square. (Then, alas, I had to redo nearly all of that fill, because when I tried arranging the bars in the top grid, I found that the best way of doing that required reversing the direction of the main grid's T1-T2-T3 rotation.) Then I filled the top grid, and (much more quickly) constructed the left and top-left grid, and that was that.
